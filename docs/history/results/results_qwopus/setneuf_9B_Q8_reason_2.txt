1.  **Understand the Goal:** The objective is to determine if a team can finish a 200-point backlog within 3 sprints given their historical velocity, and if not, calculate the minimum required velocity and the difference from their average.

2.  **Identify Given Data:**
    *   Sprint Capacity: 40 points/sprint (This might be a distractor or a constraint, but let's check it later).
    *   Remaining Backlog: 200 points.
    *   Sprints Left: 3.
    *   Historical Velocity: 35, 42, 38.
    *   Average Velocity: 38.3 (Let's verify this calculation: $(35 + 42 + 38) / 3 = 115 / 3 \approx 38.33$).

3.  **Step 1: Check if they can finish with current average velocity.**
    *   Current Average Velocity $\approx 38.33$.
    *   Total Work with current velocity = $38.33 \times 3 = 115$ points.
    *   Is $115 \ge 200$? No. They cannot finish.

4.  **Step 2: Calculate the Minimum Required Velocity.**
    *   Target Points: 200.
    *   Number of Sprints: 3.
    *   Required Velocity = $200 / 3$.
    *   Arithmetic: $200 / 3 = 66.666...$ points per sprint.

5.  **Step 3: Calculate the Difference between Required Velocity and Average Velocity.**
    *   Required Velocity: $66.67$ (rounded to two decimal places).
    *   Average Velocity: $38.33$ (rounded to two decimal places).
    *   Difference: $66.67 - 38.33 = 28.34$.

    *   "Sprint capacity of 40 story points per sprint." Does this mean they physically *cannot* do more than 40 points per sprint?
        *   If yes, then even if they want to do 66.67 points per sprint, they are capped at 40.
        *   Let's re-read carefully: "A sprint has capacity of 40 story points per sprint." This usually means the maximum amount of work they can take on.
        *   If the required velocity is 66.67, and the capacity is 40, they literally cannot achieve the required velocity because they cannot pull more than 40 points
